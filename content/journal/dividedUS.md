Division in America

I'm increasingly concerned by what a bad mood most Americans seem to be in.  In this post, I'll try to come up with some ideas about why America is so divided.

Our physical environments make us more divided.  I never thought the suburbs were the healthiest environment.  Sure, they are safe and relatively clean, but they lack the ability to help people connect.  This is probably why we've seen such a generational shift to urban enviornments: children who grew up in suburbs realize the benefits of living in a more mixed community.  

I've always liked to go walking to clear my head, and I remember as a teenager wandering through the sidewalkless streets of my neighborhood, and I intuitively felt like something was missing, but couldn't quite put my finger on it.  Maybe we were particularly antisocial, but we barely knew our nextdoor neighbors, and the entire rest of the maze of houses was just a big desert of houses. I remember how elated I felt when one day after walking for hours, on the outskirts of the development I found a convience store.  Why was I so excited to find a stupid Stewarts shop at the end of the road?  It was something differet, and it was a place where I had permission to interact with other people and our economy.  It was a trading post at the edge of the desert.

Where I currently live in Berkeley, the physical community makes it at least somewhat difficult.  I still don't know most of the neighbors who live in my apartment (perhaps that could be changed by having more common areas that are acceptable for hanging out in).  But if I'm feeling grouchy, I can go for a walk on sidewalked streets.  I can cal up a friend and ask them to meet me at a restaruant or cafe that we can both walk to within 15 minutes.  If I walk outside, there  are people around in cafes and restaurants.  In addition to the UC campus, Berkeley has many beautiful outoor parks and nature preserves to explore.  And even more uniquely, even in the more "suburban areas" of Berkeley, there are walking paths cutting between houses. The downtown area, also within a 15 minute, is a hub of activity and provides all the products and services I could imagine needing. 

For me personally, even waving a passing "hi" to a neighbor makes me feel less anonymous and in a better mood.  I'm an introverted person; I like privacy and being alone.  But I also like to feel secure and part of something.   

Being alone makes us feel less secure.  It makes us want to hoard our resources and in turn takes resources out of the towns where we live.

Americans live surprisingly lonely lives.
